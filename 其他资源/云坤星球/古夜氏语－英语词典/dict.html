Convention

Numerics can directly follow a n. or pron. as adj.. For example, “the forty of us” is “NKJ T/K”.

“/Z-” can refer to the subject of the parent clause or even it grand-parent clause based on context.

“-D” can only be used in place of NK when it has no following adj. or prep.. “-D” cannot be used after a v. with “/Z-” prefix as an object.

“-Z” cannot be used in place of /HK as subject when the sentence is not imperative. For example, “GTN NCS /HK NK?”

“-J” is only needed for large group of things or people. For small numbers like “three friends”, both with “-J” and without “-J” are correct.

B is used when the divide between subject and object is ambiguous. This is compulsory when the object is a number.

When BT is used as prep. to connect more than two things, each thing need to be preceded by a BT, starting from the second.

BPNT is used in place of “BPL BPL” to mean that “something can / may be possible”, or “maybe someone is capable of something”. \BPNT means impossible, the same meaning as \BPL, hence \BPNT is rarely used.

LSM, LSX, LM, LS are followed by K and the clan name of a person (for Goyichian nobles, end with “-C”) to address people formally. LM can be used in place of LSM in case where age or rank is not clear.

KCN and KCNT are interchangeable when the question is a sentence with v. /G. In this case, abbr. KCNG is often used.

PLN is often used in imperative sentences after “-Z” to show sincerity.

SL/ can be followed by P and a time as greetings for a specific time. For example, good morning is “SL/ P NG”.

When S/ is used as adv., it can only follow -CN or a prep. to indicate all possibilities. For example, “P S/…” means “every time when …”.

Future hopes after S//N do not need future tense indicator L\T.

When N/T or /DS is the last word of a sentence, it can be omitted in non-formal context.

When “JL-” words refer to things questioned by KCN or KCNT, they can be omitted in non-formal context.

/G does not accept adj. as object. Most adj. can themselves use as v. to describe the subject.

In different context, /DN and /DG may either refer to the subject / object of the whole previous sentence, or that of a clause in it. /DG can also be used to refer to the object of a prep..
